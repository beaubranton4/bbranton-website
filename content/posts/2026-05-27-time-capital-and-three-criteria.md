May 27, 2026

Had a nice no-phone morning, which I was really happy with. Really inspired by Mark Lu, the famous solopreneur on Twitter, who has built 30-plus projects, five of which are actually making money today. And when I say actually making money, I mean 2K-plus in MRR. It really goes to show that what I've done with Dugout Edge is no joke. I should be really proud of how far I've come, because the success rate on these kinds of indie projects built by solopreneurs is really, really low.

But it gets me thinking: is solopreneurship really the path I want to take? Is it just the easy path? Or is it truly the path that I'm interested in, that fits the skill sets I think I have, that I have the best chance at succeeding on — versus raising money, raising capital, spending capital? For me, I think solopreneurship represents a safe route. It's very hard to fail, at least at the scale at which I'm playing — low-cost businesses.

But I think if I'm going to stick down the solopreneur route, I need to take more risks. I need to invest money. Instead of just investing time. While it's proven that I can compound and build using my own time, I'm not going to live to 200 or 300 years old. It's taken me a year to get to a few K in MRR, and that's probably all I'm going to get to this year. I don't see this skyrocketing to much more. If I continue down the current path, I can keep investing time and Dugout Edge is going to grow at a linear rate. But in order to accelerate the speed at which I grow as a function of time, I'm going to need to find more leverage points: deploy more capital, deploy more labor, deploy more technology.

I think technology has been deployed to the max — I can obviously optimize and do a little bit more on that front. The next remaining leverage pool is the deployment of capital. Some ways I can do that: hire content creators. The business pretty much runs on its own, but I need to grow it. I need to get it in front of many more users. SEO is pulling 99% of the weight. And I need to start branching out into new projects.

My focus is honed in again, but sometimes I just need to put feelers out there, see what else is around — just to make sure that what I'm working on is the correct thing, the right thing I want to be working on. I'm not sure it is. And it's okay to pursue different things. That's the fun thing about solopreneurship: all of these little digital tools I build are kind of set-and-forget. They run on autopilot. They're probably not going to grow much without my intervention, and will eventually start to decline. But that allows me the freedom to pursue something else — versus if I was VC-funded, or had put in a $100,000 investment, that's just something you can't easily walk away from to go build a micro-SaaS around renting coolers, or a blog for solopreneurs.

So I'm in a good spot. I do have an interest in this idea of solopreneurship. I'm going to do a brainstorming session later in the day to write down the true, definitive, concrete ideas I can start working on. And they must fit three criteria:

1. They must solve a problem that I'm familiar with. The more intimate I am with the problem, the better.
2. It must be a problem that others are already attempting to solve. That's pre-market validation — I don't need to do much research there.
3. Is it shareable? Does the product market itself? By that I mean: is the solution something that will capture the eyes and hearts of people in an instant, with a quick video or a screenshot? Is it in a space that is visually appealing?

That last one really interests me — being able to go viral on TikTok and market on social media, which are inherently all visual today. That feels super important to me, unless I can find some other wedge. I'm kind of losing faith in the SEO wedge. I don't think agents are just going to start discovering things on their own — but maybe that's just SEO. So maybe SEO is a wedge, I'm not sure. But for now, shareability — the product marketing itself — is the key finding.

For now, just writing these down. But I'll need help eventually turning this into a concrete list of business ideas through a proper brainstorming session.
