March 1, 2026

To poor people, money means so much more than it does to rich people. Why does it mean so much to poor people? Because money is their hard-earned time. Why does money not matter to rich people? Because money buys rich people time. And it also buys more money.

Sunny days in SF are cherished more than sunny days in Hawaii. It's the scarcity.

Poor people trade time for money—hourly wages, physical labor, constrained schedules—so money literally represents their life energy. Every dollar is hours they can't get back. Rich people trade money for time—hiring help, buying convenience, passive income—so money becomes a tool to create more freedom and more money. It's leverage, not just compensation.

The scarcity principle with SF sunny days is perfect. When something is rare, each instance feels more valuable. Hawaii sun is abundant, so it's taken for granted. SF sun is precious because it's unpredictable.

Same logic applies to a lot of things: why people in small towns treasure visiting the city vs. city people taking it for granted, why someone's first $1,000 feels huge but their 100th doesn't, why free time feels more valuable when you have a demanding job vs. when you're unemployed.

The scarcity creates the meaning. Abundance creates indifference.

People want the best. They don't want the brand who has the best marketing or the best SEO skills, or who has the most money to buy ad spots. They want THE BEST.
