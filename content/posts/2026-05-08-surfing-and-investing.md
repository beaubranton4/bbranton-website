## Surfing and investing

The whole name of the game is *don't wipe out.* Don't crash out. As long as you've got your board and you're in the ocean, you're still in the game. That's rule one of investing too — survive. Stay solvent, stay liquid, stay in the water. The people who blow up don't get to come back the next day.

**Once you commit to a wave, you're on that wave.** You can't ride two waves at once. That's capital allocation. The dollar you put on this wave is not on that other wave. The conviction has to come *before* you paddle, because once you're standing up, you're committed.

**Bigger wave, bigger chance of a wipeout. Smaller wave, smaller chance — but less of a thrill, less of a win.** That's the entire risk-return curve in one image. The temptation in investing is to either chase only the biggest waves (and eat the wipeouts) or only the smallest (and never make anything worth talking about). The art is matching the wave size to your skill, your conditions, and your stage in the session.

**Many small waves vs. a few big waves.** You can spend a session catching everything that comes through — lots of action, lots of motion, but you tire yourself out and your best wave of the day might pass while you're paddling back. Or you can sit, wait, and pick. Both are valid styles. Just know which one you're playing.

**Waves come in sets. The first wave is often not the biggest.** This is the one I think about the most. The first wave looks tempting because it's the first one in a while. But the second or third in the set is usually bigger. In investing, the first move into a theme is rarely the best one — there's a tendency to grab the first thing that catches your eye and miss the real one behind it. Sit on the shoulder, let the first wave pass, and read the set.

**Crowds.** If there are too many people on a wave, you can't go. You're looking for the wave with as few people as possible. The best moment of all is being the only one on a big one. Same with investing — the asymmetry lives where nobody's looking yet. Crowded trades are crowded waves.

**You can't surf without a wave.** No volatility, no motion, no money. Sometimes the ocean just goes flat and the right move is to sit on the beach. There's no shame in not paddling out when there's nothing to ride.

**Sometimes you're just in the wrong surf spot.** No matter how patient you are or how good your form is, the wave isn't coming to where you're sitting. Move spots. Different markets, different sectors, different vehicles. Don't fall in love with where you've been sitting.

**Timing matters.** If you go too early, you get whitewashed — tired, wet, slightly off your line, and the real wave comes through while you're paddling back. You might win a little, but really that's time and energy you could have spent catching another wave at the right time and riding it out fully. If you go too late, you miss it entirely. You might get sent over the top, nose dive, or just be left with nothing to show for it.

**But if you catch it right on time — the perfect moment — the momentum sweeps you up and you just ride.** No thoughts of getting off early. The wave just *takes* you. You naturally slow down and naturally feel when the time is right to get off.

**The exit matters too.** Catch the wave too long and you ride out way past where the waves break. Now you've got a long paddle back, and you'll probably miss a whole bunch of other waves while you're paddling. The optimal exit is the one where you've ridden long enough to get the most out of the wave, but not so far out that you can't paddle back into position. You also don't want to paddle back right into the next wave breaking on your head.

That's the whole thing. Survive the wipeout. Commit to one wave at a time. Match wave size to stage. Wait for the set, not the first one. Avoid crowds. Need volatility. Be in the right spot. Time the entry. Time the exit. And ride the ones you catch all the way until the wave naturally tells you it's done.

Worth keeping with me.
