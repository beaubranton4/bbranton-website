June 1, 2026

First of the month. Spent today chasing down a business idea that's been rattling around since the Hawaii trip — selling fruit online that's in season, sourced from wherever it's at peak. Apples from the best US growers during apple season, oranges when it's orange season, and shipping exotic stuff to places that just can't get it. We're spoiled here in California. In Hawaii half the fruit you want is either not there or imported and stupid expensive. Felt like an obvious arbitrage — take the surplus from where it's abundant and sell it at a premium where it's scarce.

So I ran the keyword research on it. And the data actually backed up the instinct, which surprised me. "Exotic fruit near me" pulls 1,900 searches a month, "exotic fruit box" around 1,000, plus a whole long tail of "where to buy exotic fruit" and "...near me" — competition basically zero. That long tail is literally my Hawaii observation showing up as search behavior. People are out there searching locally, not finding it, and getting stuck. The pain is real and you can reach it. The model the keywords point to is a curated seasonal box — "here's what's at peak right now" — not a general fruit marketplace.

So far so good. Then I made myself do the honest gut-check, and that's where it got uncomfortable.

This is not a software business. It's a logistics and operations business wearing an SEO costume. Everything else I've been excited about lately is the opposite of this — micro-SaaS, near-zero marginal cost, ships in a weekend, can't be eaten by AI, take-rate pricing. This thing is the inversion of all of it. Every single order is perishable inventory plus cold-chain freight plus spoilage risk. The costs scale one-to-one with revenue. And the freight is the killer — a 2-day insulated box can cost $40-60, which is often more than the fruit inside is worth. The apple version of the idea dies right there: I'd be competing with Costco at $1.50 a pound while eating $40 of shipping. The margin only lives on scarcity — rare, exotic, peak-only fruit a market genuinely can't get. That's the only place there's pricing power.

But the thing that really stopped me was asking: where would I actually have to spend my time?

And the answer is almost entirely in the stuff I have no edge in and probably wouldn't love. Sourcing — building relationships with farmers, the calls, the travel, the negotiation. Packaging and cold-chain R&D, figuring out how to make fruit arrive not-rotten, picking it under-ripe so it peaks on arrival, gel packs, ethylene absorbers. Fulfillment — packing boxes, wrangling a 3PL, negotiating freight rates. Customer service and refunds when boxes show up damaged, which with perishables is constant. The SEO and the website — the part I'm genuinely good at and actually enjoy — is maybe 15% of the work. The other 85% is operations grind.

That's the whole finding, really. I keep saying I want a business that plays to my unfair advantages — speed, building, SEO, shipping things fast and cheap. This idea uses about 15% of that and asks me to become a perishable-goods operator for the rest. Shiny object syndrome again. The idea is genuinely good and the demand is genuinely there. It's just a fundamentally different kind of bet than the one I keep telling myself I want to make.

I'm not fully killing it. There are real ways the math could work — sell pre-orders before you buy any inventory (no spoilage risk, no demand guessing), stay regional instead of shipping cross-country, run it as a subscription so the volume is predictable, and frame it as a premium experience so a $40 freight charge disappears inside a $120 box instead of sinking a $25 order. Those are real levers. But before I'd touch any of it, there's one paper exercise that's a hard yes/no gate: price one real box end-to-end — one fruit, one destination, actual freight quote, actual wholesale cost — and see if a price people would gladly pay still leaves 30% after a spoilage reserve. And stand up a pre-order page for a single drop to see if anyone actually puts money down before I buy a single mango.

If the numbers clear, maybe. If freight and spoilage eat the margin — which honestly feels like the base case — then it's a great-sounding idea that the unit economics quietly kill, and I'll have spent zero dollars learning that.

Filed the full breakdown in the explorations folder. For now this goes in the "validated demand, killed by economics and by the fact that it's not the kind of work I want to do" pile. Back to the tools.
