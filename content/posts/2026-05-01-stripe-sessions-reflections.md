May 1, 2026

Yesterday concluded the Stripe Sessions conference that I attended, a two-day conference in San Francisco hosted by Stripe. As I mentioned in my previous journal entry, I was reached out to by my Stripe partner to attend and got to attend the event for free while others paid $1,000 just to get entry. It was held in the Moscone Center where legendary announcements were made, such as when Steve Jobs announced the iPhone and many others.

Reflecting back on the conference, I'll start with yesterday. It began with the keynote. Speakers John and Patrick Collison, the founders of Stripe, talked about the economy through what they were seeing with Stripe data. Stripe makes up 1.6% of global GDP—or rather, 1.6% of global GDP flows through Stripe's payment rails. So they have a really good understanding of who's building, what's happening, some of the trends taking shape in today's world.

Some of the biggest trends are that agents are taking over. More and more people are able to start businesses as they're enabled by agents. There are spikes in the number of businesses created, solopreneurs creating businesses, businesses joining certain Stripe programs created specifically for solopreneurs. And the fact that productivity gains have yet to be seen, but they're going to take years because the infrastructure for AI and AI agents isn't yet built out. It's going to take time.

As I reflect, I kept thinking to myself: should I be jumping in on this trend? Right now I'm in on the trend because I'm leveraging the technology, but should I be contributing to the ecosystem? That's the question I have.

And I think what I've determined for myself is that I love movement, I love sports, I love being social, I love being outdoors, I love playing. And I would love to help others do the same while taking my interests in technology and AI and automation—bringing those all together to help others do those things that I love. To help bring people together, to help them play, to help them enjoy social events. So I think that's where I can really contribute to the world, at this intersection of all of these things. Because I'm fascinated by these technologies. I love thinking about the applications of these technologies. But I'm not super interested in building out or creating these technologies. Or maybe I'm just fearful of it, I'm not sure. So right now, that is where my head is at.

After all, I've got the perfect profile. I was a Stanford athlete, a Stanford baseball player. I worked at Disneyland supporting in-person operations. I have a background in data. I've built a company using AI. I've built multiple businesses with the help of AI, leveraging AI. I've built many automations. And at the same time, I'm in really good shape. I'm a very social person. And I think that all of these things have culminated into this essence of who I am and who I want to be and how I can provide value and help others in this world.

Ultimately, I need to ask myself: what are the deepest problems? And I think that people don't have ways of getting out into the world. People don't have ways of meeting new people and getting active and joining team events and participating in these fun activities, these games, these playful moments. That we all seem to miss out on as we age and get older and we spend our time working, sitting behind desks and talking to computers.
