I found out today from my dad that my mom was diagnosed with Alzheimer's dementia. We don't know the full diagnosis because this is coming from my mom, who then told my dad, who then told me, and I think along those lines of communication there's probably something missing.

But my dad found out that my mom had secretly been seeing a neurologist behind all of our backs. "Behind all of our backs" might be the wrong way to phrase it — it's really just that she was going to see a neurologist in secret. But I'm glad I went back home when I did and had the discussions that I did, because we had tried to make an appointment for her. She cancelled the appointment. So she's sharp enough to know that she's already been seeing someone, and she's had these appointments set up.

And today it was finally confirmed — or maybe not today, but earlier this week — it was confirmed that she does indeed have it and that it is serious. How serious remains to be seen. But I think we are about to find out.

I just am overcome with emotions, namely sadness for my mom, but for my dad, for our family. I know that we'll get through it. But it's hard to think that I've finally reached the stage of my life that I watched my own parents go through. And now I think the realization has hit me that I am at the beginning stages of going through that as well.

There are some medicines that have shown positive signs of slowing the progression of Alzheimer's, called lecanemab. I might be pronouncing that wrong. But I think the goal here is to get my mom through that screening process and get her access to the drug, so that we have the best shot of prolonging her life — prolonging her abilities and her mental state for as long as we can.

The saddest thing to me is that she spent — gosh, probably 10 to 15 years of her life caring for both of her parents. First her dad, who had Alzheimer's and dementia, and then her mom, who also had it. Taking care of both of them, I want to say she spent 15 years of her life looking after people with Alzheimer's. And as soon as her mom passed away, which was in 2022, she started to show signs of getting the disease herself.

That's what's truly sad — that for however long she lives, and I think there's still a very good chance that's another 10, 15, 20, 30 years, almost 40 to 50% of her life will have been spent dealing with this disease called Alzheimer's and dementia.
