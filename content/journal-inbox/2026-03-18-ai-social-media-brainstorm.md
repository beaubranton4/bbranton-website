## WE ALL WANT TO BE REMEMBERED

Give people an infrastructure to create an AI version of themselves. It's basically a structured database and an AI that is trained a specific way on those sorts of data points. Pictures, images, writings. The more you give it, the more it interacts.

This could be a whole world. This could be the new social media. We do the creation of your avatar and give it a world. It does the rest. Every "profile" is its own AI agent trained on their owner.

---

## Thoughts on the idea

This is a genuinely compelling idea, and the core insight — that people's deepest online motivation is legacy, not connection — is sharp. Most social media is about being seen now. You're talking about existing forever. That's a different and arguably more powerful emotional pull.

### What's strong

- The "feed it more, get more back" mechanic is psychology. It creates a natural engagement loop that doesn't feel extractive — it feels like an investment in yourself
- The posthumous use case almost sells itself. Families would pay real money for this
- "Every profile is its own agent" is genuinely novel framing vs. static memorial pages like what Facebook already does badly

### Where it gets complicated

The hardest problem isn't technical — it's consent and identity drift. Once someone dies, who controls the AI? Who can it talk to? Can it say things the person never would have? These become deeply emotional legal and ethical landmines fast.

The second hardest problem is the uncanny valley of personality. A photo album is clearly a photo album. An AI that talks like your dead dad but gets things slightly wrong might be more disturbing than comforting for many people.

### The real strategic question

Is this a legacy product (focused on death/memory) or a living identity product (your AI self exists while you're alive and represents you)? Those are actually two different businesses with different customers, different monetization, and different emotional contexts. The living version might be the smarter wedge — less morbid, easier to acquire users, and the legacy use case comes naturally over time.

### Honest competitive concern

This space is getting crowded fast. HereAfter AI, StoryFile, and others are already here. The moat isn't the AI — it's the data accumulation and the world you build around it. That "world" piece you mentioned at the end is actually the most interesting and least developed part of your pitch. What does that world look like? That might be where the real idea lives.

## The Social Media Angle

### The core shift

Right now, social media is you performing for an audience. You post, people react, you post more. The platform benefits from your anxiety about engagement.

What you're describing is your AI living on your behalf. You stop performing. Your agent performs. That's a radical inversion.

### What makes it potentially huge

People are exhausted by social media but don't actually want to disappear. They want presence without the labor. Your agent could respond to people, represent your taste, hold conversations — while you're asleep, working, living your life. That tension between "I want to be known" and "I'm tired of posting" is real and unsolved.

The training loop is also genuinely addictive in a healthier way. Instead of chasing likes, you're building something. Feeding it journal entries, voice memos, photos, opinions. It feels productive rather than hollow.

### The hard design questions

- Do people interact with your agent, or does your agent interact with their agent? Agent-to-agent social graphs are something nobody has really cracked yet and it's fascinating
- How transparent is it? Does someone know they're talking to your AI vs. you?
- What's the "post" equivalent? What's the unit of content in this world?

### The real unlock

The data network effect here is brutal for competitors to replicate. Every piece of yourself someone feeds the platform makes them harder to leave. That's stickier than any photo library.

The question is whether people trust a company enough to hand over that level of self-data. You'd need to earn that trust early and loudly.

## The Trust Insight

"People want 1,000,000 versions of themselves instead of 1,000,000 random agents. They still don't trust agents. But would they trust an agent trained on themselves?"

### The trust gap

Nobody wants to send an AI to do their job, represent their opinion, or speak to their friends because the AI might say something they wouldn't say. It's not a capability problem. It's an identity problem.

But an agent trained on you collapses that gap. It's not "an AI" anymore. It's closer to an extension of your will. You'd trust it the same way you trust your own memory or your own judgment — imperfectly, but fundamentally yours.

### The 1,000,000 versions insight

Right now people want to be in every room, every conversation, every opportunity simultaneously and can't. Your agent isn't replacing you — it's multiplying you. That's a completely different value proposition than productivity AI.

- One version of you networking at an event
- One version engaging with your audience
- One version responding to opportunities while you sleep

The ambition isn't automation. It's scale of self.

### Why this might be the actual wedge into agent adoption

Every other agent product asks you to trust a stranger. This asks you to trust yourself. That's a much easier sell. You're essentially using AI adoption anxiety as the feature — people come because they don't trust other agents.

### The interesting tension

The more accurate it becomes, the more it raises the question — at what point is it just you? And at what point does it start becoming you in ways you didn't choose?

That line is where the product gets philosophically wild. But also where it gets deeply compelling to users.
